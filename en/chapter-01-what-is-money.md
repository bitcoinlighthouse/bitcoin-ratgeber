# Chapter 1 – What is Money?

**Understanding the foundation – before you buy Bitcoin**

---

## The Idea of Money

Bitcoin is meant to be the best money that has ever existed – so many say. But what actually is money?

Money is a highly liquid medium that accelerates the exchange of labor, value, and resources between people while also serving as a tool to store and measure the value of goods and services.

| Function | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Medium of exchange | Money facilitates the direct exchange of goods and services. |
| Store of value | Money can preserve value over time – exchange does not have to happen immediately. |
| Unit of account | Money serves as a common unit of measure that makes values comparable. |

Imagine we lived on an island with a few thousand inhabitants – and the Euro didn't exist yet. What would we use as money, and why? What properties would something need to fulfill the functions of a medium of exchange, store of value, and unit of account?

Maybe a stone? Hardly. It isn't scarce enough for the baker to give me a bread roll in return. He would probably just point at the ground and say: "What do I need a stone for? I can pick one up myself."

Then perhaps something rarer – a gemstone? That sounds better. But maybe I only want to buy a bread roll, and here the next problem appears: lack of divisibility. A gemstone cannot easily be broken down into small, equal units without losing its value.

This makes it clear: money must fulfill certain properties. It should be scarce, but not too scarce. It needs to be divisible so small transactions are possible. It should be easy to transport so it stays practical in daily use. Its authenticity must be verifiable so trust can develop. It needs durability to preserve its value over time. And finally, it should be fungible – every unit should be interchangeable with every other, so it can serve as a reliable unit of account.

Only the combination of all these properties truly makes something money.

## Cowrie Shells

For thousands of years, cowrie shells were a globally accepted means of payment – from China and India to Africa. They were not state-issued money but commodity money – like gold – which derived its function from its properties:

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Scarcity | Not easily available in large quantities. |
| Portability | They were lightweight. |
| Verifiability | Counterfeiting was practically impossible. |
| Durability | Cowrie shells were extremely long-lasting. |
| Divisibility | Ideal as small change; larger amounts were strung on threads. |
| Fungibility | Every shell had the same value, regardless of size or shape. |

When European sailors flooded the market with billions of cowrie shells from the 16th to the 18th century, shell money lost its scarcity – and with it its value. Once scarcity is broken, it cannot be restored.

## The Gold Standard and Its End

For centuries, gold met many of the properties of good money: scarce, durable, universally desired. But it was difficult to transport and impractical for large trade volumes. Banks therefore began to store gold and issue paper certificates. Early dollar bills read: *"In Gold Coin Payable To The Bearer On Demand"* – the promise to redeem the note for gold at any time.

> **The Oracle Problem**  
> Every gold certificate issued creates a promise. No one outside the issuing institution can verify whether enough gold actually exists. Citizens cannot look inside the vaults – they have to trust. You can only "oracle" whether the gold exists. This design flaw made the gold standard practically impossible to uphold.

The same gold could be "promised" multiple times – as backing for several banknotes, account balances, and government bonds at the same time. This double-issuance problem and the oracle problem reinforced each other.

The gold standard did not fail because of gold – it failed because of humans. The final break came in 1971, when U.S. President Richard Nixon ended the dollar's convertibility to gold. What remained was pure paper money.

---

## The Petrodollar and Fiat Money

A central question remained after 1971: why would other countries trade goods for worthless paper? The United States negotiated with Saudi Arabia that Saudi oil would only be priced in U.S. dollars. This created an artificially generated, global demand for dollars – every country had to hold dollars in order to buy oil. The dollar was no longer backed by gold but by oil – and by power.

> **FIAT MONEY**  
> From the Latin "fiat" – "let it be done." Money that can be created out of nothing, without material backing. Euro, dollar, franc – all modern currencies are fiat money.

> **Cantillon Effect**  
> Whoever encounters newly created money first – banks, governments, large institutions – benefits the most. Whoever stands at the end of the chain bears the inflation. This explains why asset and real estate prices rise faster than wages.

## How Did Bitcoin Come About?

On October 31, 2008 – in the middle of the worst financial crisis in decades – a pseudonym named Satoshi Nakamoto published a nine-page document to a cryptography mailing list: the Bitcoin whitepaper.

> **CYPHERPUNKS**  
> A group of people who use cryptography to defend privacy against state surveillance. Since the 1980s they discussed concepts for breaking the state monopoly on money through cryptography. The decisive problem nobody could solve until then: the double-spending problem.

Satoshi Nakamoto's idea: a public, distributed ledger running on thousands of computers at the same time. Every transaction visible to everyone, immutable, and unambiguous. Whoever provides computing power to secure the network is rewarded – there is no other way to create new Bitcoin.

## The Oracle Problem and the Double-Spending Problem – Solved

### The Park Bench Story

Imagine you're sitting with Paul on a park bench. Paul wants an ice cream and says he could pay later – he has plenty of gold at home. You can't check. You have to trust. That is the oracle problem: someone has to testify that a value really exists – and you can't verify it yourself.

Then Paul realizes he does have cash on him after all. He hands you a banknote. Now you have it – Paul doesn't. Ownership has clearly shifted, without a third party, without any promise. That is exactly why cash works so well: it is physical. It can only be in one place at a time.

But what if Paul sent you something digital instead – a file, a number, a token? Here the second problem begins. A digital file can be copied. If Paul sends you a file: how do you know it only belongs to you? That he hasn't kept copies? That he hasn't sent it to five other people at the same time? That is the double-spending problem – and it is the reason there was no functioning digital cash before Bitcoin.

The classic attempt at a solution was a central ledger – like a bank. The bank keeps track of who owns what. Problem solved? Only in appearance. Because now the operator of the ledger can create more units, demands trust – and we are back at the oracle problem.

> **Bitcoin's Answer**  
> What if nobody controlled the ledger – and it still worked? Bitcoin solves this through a public, distributed ledger running on thousands of computers. Ownership is unambiguous. Double-spending is impossible. No third party has to demand trust.

## Proof of Work – Trust Through Labor

Every previous money system fails at the same point: someone has to guarantee that the rules are followed. Bitcoin solves this differently – not through promises, but through verifiable, real work.

Miners are not "money printers" but accountants with costs. They bundle transactions, verify them, and write them immutably into the public ledger. To do so, they must solve a cryptographic puzzle – easy to verify, extremely expensive to forge.

> **The Decisive Point**  
> Cheating costs more energy than being honest. Anyone who wanted to cheat would have to bring more computing power to bear than the rest of the network combined – permanently, with no guarantee of success. Manipulation becomes economically irrational.

In the Bitcoin system, you don't have to trust anyone – not a bank, not a state, not even the miners. All rules are public, all transactions are inspectable, anyone can verify for themselves.

> *"Bitcoin doesn't say: trust me. Bitcoin says: verify me."* – Core principle of the Bitcoin network

## Hard Money and Soft Money

Give me 100 Euros today. In ten years, the same number will still be on the banknote – but you will probably only be able to buy half as much with it. That is soft money: arbitrarily expandable, with falling purchasing power.

Hard money cannot simply be increased. Other cryptocurrencies are nothing more than fiat money with another name – any computer scientist can create one and change the supply. With Bitcoin, no one can expand the money supply. It is always 21 million – set for eternity.

> **SOUND MONEY**  
> Sound or hard money with fixed, or at least very high, scarcity. The opposite of fiat money, which can be created out of nothing.
